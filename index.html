Methodology

  • Routing algorithm. Travel times are computed by Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm running over a multi-modal graph for each chosen origin. The graph has roughly 9,200 nodes: a 70 by 130 grid of cells covering the island plus one node per modeled subway stop. Edges encode walking, driving, boarding, riding, alighting, and transfer transitions, each with a fixed cost in minutes.
  • Spatial discretization. Manhattan is tiled into a grid where each cell represents approximately 150 meters on a side. Cells outside the hand-traced Manhattan polygon are excluded from the graph. The polygon is a simplified approximation of the actual coastline.
  • Walking. Modeled at a uniform 3 mph (4.8 km/h) along the grid. The grid is 4-connected (up, down, left, right), which approximates walking along Manhattan's street and avenue grid. No allowance is made for terrain, sidewalk congestion, or signal-controlled crossings.
  • Driving. Each cell is classified into one of three road types: the Midtown core, all other Manhattan surface streets, and the FDR Drive / West Side Highway corridors. Speeds for each class vary with the selected time-of-day window: Midtown ranges from 3.5 mph at PM rush to 12 mph late at night; other surface streets from 6 to 16 mph; the highways from 20 to 45 mph. Speed values are hand-curated from published NYC TLC trip-record averages and NYC DOT speed studies; real-time traffic feeds are not consulted. A 4-minute fixed overhead at the origin represents parking and access time.
  • Biking. The same three road classes drive bike speeds: ~10 mph on most surface streets, ~7 mph through the Midtown core during the day, and ~14 mph along the Hudson and East River Greenways (which share corridors with the WSH and FDR). Biking is less sensitive to traffic than driving, but speeds dip slightly at rush hour due to pedestrian and intersection congestion. A 1-minute fixed overhead at the origin represents grabbing a Citi Bike or unlocking your own. Bike infrastructure attributes are simplified to corridor-vs-street; specific protected lanes on individual avenues are not modeled. Speed values are drawn from aggregated Citi Bike trip-level averages and the NACTO Cycling Design Guide.
  • Time of day. Five canonical windows are modeled: late night (1–5am), AM rush (7–10am), midday (10am–3pm), PM rush (4–7pm), and evening (7pm–1am). Driving speeds and subway platform waits both vary by window. Walking is held constant across windows. Switching windows clears the drive- and subway-mode portions of the shortest-path cache and re-runs Dijkstra. Five windows rather than 24 hours reflects the actual resolution of the underlying speed data.
  • Subway network. Approximately 55 Manhattan stations are encoded by hand, grouped by line and complex. Modeled lines include the 1, 2/3, 4/5, 6, A, C, B/D, F, N/Q/R/W, L, 7, and the Second Avenue Q. Local stops are sometimes folded into nearby express stops to reduce data volume.
  • Subway timing. Inter-station travel times are approximated from published MTA scheduled run times: roughly 1.4 minutes per stop on locals and 2.2 to 2.5 minutes per stop on expresses with longer skips. The platform wait on first boarding ranges from 3 minutes at rush hour to 10 minutes late at night, again varying with the time-of-day window. Cross-platform transfers between co-located lines cost 2 to 9 minutes of walking, plus the same window-dependent wait for the next train.
  • Multi-modal routing. In subway mode the optimizer is free to mix walking and riding. A typical computed route looks like: walk to the nearest station, ride a specific line some number of stops, transfer if useful, walk to the destination. The hover tooltip displays the full breakdown for any point, and the thin colored line on the map traces the route Dijkstra selected.
  • Data sources. Station coordinates and line connectivity are hand-curated from the MTA public station list. Per-stop run times are derived from posted schedules. Driving speeds are rough averages from publicly reported NYC DOT and academic studies of Manhattan traffic. The Manhattan polygon was hand-traced from open-source basemaps. No proprietary routing APIs are queried.
  • Limitations. Bus, Citi Bike, ferry, and PATH are not modeled. Wait times are not schedule-aware. Subway service changes, weekend reroutes, and elevator outages are ignored. Driving speeds are zone averages rather than block-by-block. The visualization is intended to convey the qualitative shape of New York's time geography, not to substitute for a production routing engine such as Citymapper, Google Maps, or Apple Maps.
  • Implementation. The entire model runs client-side in the browser. Dijkstra results are cached per origin and mode so repeated mode switches at the same starting point reuse the same computation. Source: a single self-contained HTML file with no external dependencies beyond Google Fonts.
